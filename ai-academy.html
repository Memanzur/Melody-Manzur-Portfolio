What is AI, Really?

Cut through the noise. Learn what AI actually is, how it works, and what it can and cannot do.

Beginner ▼

AI vs Machine Learning vs LLMs

▶

Artificial Intelligence is the big umbrella. It means any computer system that can do something that normally requires human intelligence. That includes everything from the spam filter in your email to a self-driving car.

Machine Learning is a type of AI where instead of writing rules by hand, you feed the computer a bunch of examples and let it figure out the patterns itself. Think of it like teaching a kid to recognize dogs by showing them thousands of pictures of dogs instead of describing what a dog looks like.

Large Language Models (LLMs) are a specific kind of machine learning. These are the models behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. They were trained on enormous amounts of text from the internet. Books, articles, code, conversations. They learned patterns in language so well that they can generate new text that sounds human.

The key thing to understand: LLMs do not "think" the way you do. They predict the most likely next word based on patterns they learned during training. That prediction is so good it can feel like intelligence, but it is pattern matching at a massive scale.

How ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini Actually Work

▶

All three of these tools are built on the same core idea called a transformer. Here is the simplified version of how it works:

Step 1: Training. The model reads billions of pages of text and learns statistical relationships between words. "The cat sat on the ___" probably ends with "mat" or "couch," not "airplane." It does this for every possible combination of words, at every level of complexity.

Step 2: You type a prompt. When you ask Claude a question, it takes your entire message, processes it through layers of math, and starts generating a response one word at a time. Each word is chosen based on probability. "What is the most likely next word given everything that came before?"

Step 3: Context window. The model can only "see" a limited amount of text at once. This is called the context window. Think of it like a desk. You can only spread out so many papers before things start falling off. Longer conversations or bigger documents eat into that space.

This is why AI sometimes "forgets" what you said earlier in a long conversation. It literally ran out of desk space.

What AI Can and Cannot Do

▶

AI is great at:

Writing and editing text. Summarizing long documents. Explaining concepts in simple terms. Writing code. Brainstorming ideas. Translating languages. Analyzing data you give it. Following instructions.

AI is bad at:

Knowing what happened after its training data cutoff. Doing math reliably (it can get it wrong). Accessing the internet in real time (unless given tools). Being creative in a truly original way, it remixes what it learned. Knowing if something is factually true vs just likely sounding.

AI cannot:

Feel emotions. Have opinions (it simulates them). Learn from your conversation permanently (each chat starts fresh unless the tool saves it). Replace human judgment on important decisions. Guarantee accuracy on anything.

The biggest mistake people make is treating AI output as fact. Always verify important information. AI is a draft machine, not a truth machine.

Why AI Literacy is a Career Superpower

▶

Here is the reality: AI is not going to replace you. But someone who knows how to use AI will outperform someone who does not. Every time.

Think about when spreadsheets first came out. The accountants who learned Excel did not lose their jobs. They became 10x more productive and got promoted. The ones who refused to learn eventually got left behind.

AI is the same thing. It is a tool that amplifies what you already know. A marketer who uses AI can produce more content, test more ideas, and analyze more data than one who does everything manually. A developer who uses AI can ship features faster. A student who uses AI can research deeper and write better.

The people who will win are the ones who learn how AI works well enough to know when to trust it, when to double check it, and when to ignore it completely.

That is exactly what The Melody Method teaches you. By the end of this course, you will not just use AI. You will understand it well enough to make it work for your specific goals.

Using AI Every Day

Stop asking AI bad questions and getting bad answers. Learn prompt engineering and which AI to use when.

Beginner ▼

Prompt Engineering Fundamentals

▶

The quality of what AI gives you depends entirely on how you ask. This is called prompt engineering and it is the single most important AI skill you can learn.

Rule 1: Be specific. "Write me a blog post" gives you generic garbage. "Write a 500 word blog post about why small businesses need SEO, targeted at restaurant owners who have never done marketing, in a casual and friendly tone" gives you something you can actually use.

Rule 2: Give context. Tell the AI who you are, what the situation is, and what you need the output for. "I'm a freelance designer pitching a new client. Help me write a proposal" works way better than "write a proposal."

Rule 3: Give examples. If you want a specific style or format, show the AI what good looks like. Paste in a sample and say "write something in this style."

Rule 4: Iterate. Your first prompt rarely gives the best result. Treat it like a conversation. "That's good but make it shorter." "Remove the jargon." "Add a call to action at the end." Each round gets better.

Think of prompting like giving instructions to a very smart intern who just started today. They are capable of great work but they know nothing about your specific situation. The more context you give, the better they perform.

Claude vs ChatGPT vs Gemini: Which AI for What

▶

These three are not the same tool. They have different strengths and knowing which one to reach for is a real skill.

Claude (Anthropic)

Best for: long documents, careful analysis, coding, following complex instructions. Tends to be more thoughtful and less likely to make things up. Great for anything where accuracy matters more than speed.

ChatGPT (OpenAI)

Best for: brainstorming, creative writing, quick answers, general knowledge. Has a huge plugin ecosystem and image generation built in. The go-to for everyday casual use.

Gemini (Google)

Best for: research that needs current info, anything tied to Google services, multimodal tasks with images and video. Strong when you need Google Search integrated into AI answers.

My personal workflow: I use Claude for anything serious, coding, long writing, analysis. ChatGPT for quick brainstorms and creative stuff. Gemini when I need something that requires up-to-date search results.

AI for Writing, Research, Coding, and Analysis

▶

Writing: Use AI as a first draft machine, not a finished product machine. Get a rough draft fast, then rewrite it in your voice. Ask it to "write three different versions" so you can pick the best parts from each. Never publish AI text without editing it yourself.

Research: AI is great at explaining concepts and summarizing information, but always cross-reference important facts. Ask it to cite sources when possible. Use it to understand topics faster, not as your only source of truth.

Coding: This is where AI really shines. Describe what you want to build in plain English and AI can write the code. But here is the catch: if you do not understand what the code does, you will not be able to fix it when it breaks. Use AI to code faster, not to skip learning.

Analysis: Paste in data, spreadsheets, or reports and ask AI to find patterns, summarize findings, or suggest next steps. This is one of the highest value uses of AI because it turns hours of manual work into minutes.

Building Your Personal AI Workflow

▶

The goal is not to use AI for everything. The goal is to figure out where AI saves you the most time and build habits around those use cases.

Step 1: Write down the 5 tasks you do most often in your work. Email, reports, research, content creation, whatever it is.

Step 2: For each task, try using AI to do 80% of the work. Time yourself. If AI saves you at least 30 minutes per week on that task, it is worth building into your routine.

Step 3: Save your best prompts. Create a document with prompts that consistently give you good results. This becomes your personal prompt library. You will reuse these constantly.

Step 4: Review monthly. What is working? What is wasting your time? Kill the workflows that do not save time and double down on the ones that do.

The people who get the most out of AI are not the ones who use it the most. They are the ones who use it strategically for the right tasks.

AI-Powered Marketing and Content Creation

▶

Content is the engine of modern marketing and AI makes it possible to produce more of it without sacrificing quality. Here is how I use it with my own clients.

Social media: I use AI to generate post ideas, write captions, and plan content calendars. I give it the brand voice, the target audience, and the goal for each post. Then I edit and add the personal touch. A week's worth of social content that used to take me a full day now takes about two hours.

SEO content: I use AI to research keywords, outline blog posts, and write first drafts. Then I rewrite to add personal experience, real examples, and the brand's personality. Google can detect pure AI content and will not rank it well. The human layer is what makes it work.

Email marketing: Subject lines, body copy, CTAs. AI can write ten versions and I pick the best one. Split testing with AI written variants has consistently improved open rates for my clients.

The key to using AI in marketing: AI generates the raw material, you add the strategy and personality. Brands that publish raw AI content all sound the same. That is not marketing, that is noise.

Automating Repetitive Business Tasks

▶

The fastest ROI from AI comes from automating the things you do over and over.

Client onboarding: Create a template prompt that takes a new client's info and generates their welcome email, project timeline, and first set of questions. What used to take 45 minutes now takes 5.

Invoice and proposal generation: Feed AI your pricing structure and the scope of work. It drafts the proposal. You review and send. This alone saves hours per week if you handle multiple clients.

Meeting summaries: Record your meetings (with permission), transcribe them, and have AI pull out action items, decisions made, and follow-up tasks. Never lose track of what was agreed on.

Data entry and formatting: Got messy data from a client? Paste it into AI and ask it to clean, organize, and format it. This works shockingly well for things like contact lists, product catalogs, and spreadsheet cleanup.

Using AI for Client Deliverables

▶

Here is a real example from my own business. When I took on Little Red Esthetics as a client, I used AI at every stage:

Research: I had AI analyze competitor med spa websites and pull out what they did well and what they missed. This gave me a competitive edge before I wrote a single line of code.

Copywriting: I used AI to draft website copy, then rewrote every section to match the client's voice and brand. The client got professional copy that sounds like them, not like a robot.

Design direction: I described the brand aesthetic to AI and it helped me generate color palette ideas, layout suggestions, and content hierarchy recommendations.

SEO setup: AI helped me research local keywords, write meta descriptions, and create a site structure that ranks. The technical SEO stuff that would take hours of manual research.

I went from a broken single-page template to an 8-page site with interactive features, SEO, and booking integration. AI did not build the site for me. It made me faster at every step so I could deliver more in less time.

When to Use AI and When Not To

▶

Use AI when:

You need a first draft fast. You are doing repetitive work. You need to brainstorm or explore ideas. You are working with data that needs to be organized. You need to explain something in simpler terms. You are coding something you understand but just need written faster.

Do not use AI when:

Accuracy is critical and you cannot verify the output (legal documents, medical advice, financial reporting). When the work requires deep personal experience that AI cannot have. When you are making a decision that affects people's lives or livelihoods. When you need truly original creative vision, not remixed patterns.

Never use AI to:

Replace your own judgment on important decisions. Generate content you publish without reading. Fake expertise you do not have. Do work you do not understand well enough to check. Handle sensitive client data without their knowledge and consent.

AI is a power tool. Like any power tool, it is incredibly useful in the right hands and dangerous if you do not know what you are doing. Learn the tool, respect its limits, and use it responsibly.

Building AI Agents

Go from using AI to building with it. Learn how agents work and what makes them different from chatbots.

Advanced ▼

Agents vs Chatbots: What is the Difference?

▶

A chatbot waits for you to ask a question, answers it, and waits again. It is reactive. You do all the thinking about what to do next.

An agent is different. An agent takes a goal, breaks it down into steps, uses tools to complete each step, and keeps going until the job is done. It is proactive. You give it the destination, it figures out the route.

Here is the simplest way to think about it: a chatbot is like texting a smart friend. An agent is like hiring a smart assistant who goes and does the work.

Example: You tell a chatbot "find me cheap flights to Miami." It gives you a list of suggestions. You tell an agent "book me the cheapest flight to Miami next Friday, window seat, carry-on only." It searches, compares, selects, and books it.

The shift from chatbot to agent is the shift from "AI that talks" to "AI that does." This is where AI goes from cool to genuinely useful.

How Agents Think: Tools, Memory, and Loops

▶

Every AI agent has three core components:

1. Tools. These are actions the agent can take. Read a file, search the web, write code, send an email, call an API. Without tools, an agent is just a chatbot. Tools are what let it interact with the real world.

2. Memory. Agents need to remember what they have done, what worked, and what did not. Short-term memory is the conversation context. Long-term memory can be files, databases, or notes the agent writes to itself.

3. Reasoning loops. This is the magic. The agent thinks: "What is my goal? What do I know? What should I do next?" It takes an action, observes the result, and decides the next step. This loop repeats until the goal is met or the agent decides it needs help.

Think of it like a loop: Think, Act, Observe, Repeat.

The difference between a good agent and a bad one is usually the quality of its reasoning loop. A bad agent tries random things. A good agent thinks before it acts, learns from what happens, and adapts its plan.

Real World Example: How I Built Boppi

▶

Boppi is a Chrome extension I built and published to the Chrome Web Store. It stops sensitive data from leaking into AI chatbots like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.

Here is how AI agent thinking applies to how I built it:

The problem: People paste passwords, credit card numbers, API keys, and personal info into AI chatbots without thinking. That data goes to the AI company's servers. Boppi catches that before it happens.

Agent-style building: I used Claude Code (which is itself an AI agent) to help me build Boppi. I described what I wanted the extension to do. Claude wrote code, I reviewed it, tested it, and iterated. The process was a loop: describe, build, test, fix, repeat.

Why this matters: I did not hand-write every line of code. I also did not let AI write everything without checking. The combination of my cybersecurity knowledge (knowing what data patterns to detect) plus AI's coding speed (writing the regex patterns and Chrome API calls) is what made Boppi possible in weeks instead of months.

Boppi exists because I understood the problem deeply and used AI tools to build the solution faster. That is the whole game: domain knowledge plus AI tools equals shipping real products.

The AI Slop Problem: Why Model Quality Matters

▶

Let me be straight with you about something a lot of AI courses will not tell you: not all AI models are equal, and using the wrong one will give you garbage output.

"AI slop" is what happens when people use low-end or low-reasoning models to build things. The output looks kind of right at first glance but it is full of problems. Generic copy that sounds like every other AI website. Code that works in the demo but breaks in production. Designs that scream "a robot made this."

Why does this happen? Cheaper models cut corners. They have less reasoning ability, smaller context windows, and weaker instruction-following. They are fine for simple tasks like "summarize this paragraph." But when you ask them to build something complex, they produce slop.

What does AI slop look like?

Websites with purple gradients, three-column feature grids with icons in circles, and headlines like "Unlock the Power of..." That is AI slop. Code that works for the happy path but crashes on edge cases. That is AI slop. Content that uses words like "delve" and "robust" and "comprehensive" in every paragraph. AI slop.

If you are building anything real, anything you want people to use, pay for a capable model. The free tier or the cheapest option will cost you more in the long run because you will spend hours fixing what it gets wrong. Good tools are an investment, not an expense.

Git and GitHub from Zero

▶

Git is a version control system. Think of it like an unlimited undo button for your entire project. Every time you "commit," you save a snapshot of your files. You can go back to any snapshot at any time.

GitHub is a website that hosts your Git projects (called repositories) online. It is like Google Drive for code, but with superpowers. Collaboration tools, issue tracking, project boards, and free website hosting.

The basics you need to know:

Repository (repo): A folder for your project with version history attached. Create one for each project.

Commit: A saved snapshot. Always write a message describing what you changed. "Fixed the nav bar on mobile" is good. "Updated stuff" is useless.

Push: Upload your local commits to GitHub so they are backed up and visible online.

Pull: Download changes from GitHub to your local machine. Important when collaborating.

Branch: A parallel version of your project. Work on a new feature without messing up the main version. When it is ready, merge it back.

You do not need to memorize every Git command. You need to understand the concepts: save often (commit), back up online (push), and experiment safely (branch). AI can help you with the specific commands when you need them.

Deploying Websites with GitHub Pages

▶

GitHub Pages is free website hosting, straight from your repository. This portfolio you are reading right now is hosted on GitHub Pages. So is the Little Red Esthetics website I built for my client.

How it works:

1. Create a repository on GitHub with your website files (HTML, CSS, JavaScript).

2. Go to Settings, then Pages.

3. Select the branch you want to deploy (usually "main").

4. GitHub gives you a URL like yourname.github.io/project-name.

That is it. Every time you push changes to that branch, the website updates automatically. Free hosting, free SSL (the little lock icon), and it works with custom domains if you have one.

Why this matters: You do not need to pay for hosting to launch a website. You do not need to configure servers. You push your code, GitHub serves it to the world. For portfolios, landing pages, documentation, and small business sites, this is genuinely all you need.

Every project in my portfolio is deployed on GitHub Pages. Boppi's marketing site, Little Red Esthetics, this entire Melody Method platform. Zero hosting costs.

Collaboration and Version Control Workflows

▶

Even if you work solo, version control habits will save you. Here is the workflow I use:

1. Main branch stays clean. Never work directly on main. It should always be the stable, working version of your project.

2. Feature branches for everything. Want to add a new page? Create a branch called "add-contact-page." Want to fix a bug? Create "fix-mobile-nav." Each task gets its own branch.

3. Commit often with good messages. Every time you finish a small piece of work, commit it. "Add hero section to homepage" is good. "WIP" tells you nothing when you look back at it later.

4. Merge when it is ready. When the feature works and you have tested it, merge the branch back into main. Delete the branch. Keep it clean.

If you are working with a team: Pull Requests (PRs) are how you propose changes. You create a PR, someone reviews it, and then it gets merged. Even when working solo, PRs are useful because they give you a record of what changed and why.

Building Your Public Portfolio on GitHub

▶

Your GitHub profile is your resume for the modern internet. Employers, clients, and collaborators will look at it. Here is how to make it work for you.

Pin your best repos. GitHub lets you pin 6 repositories to your profile. Choose the ones that show your best work. Quality over quantity.

Write good READMEs. Every pinned repo should have a README that explains: what the project is, why you built it, how to use it, and what technologies you used. A screenshot or demo link goes a long way.

Keep it active. A green contribution graph shows consistency. Even small commits count. Fix a typo, update a README, add a feature. Regular activity signals that you are building, not just talking about building.

Create a profile README. GitHub lets you create a special repo with your username that displays on your profile page. Use it to introduce yourself, list your skills, and link to your best work.

Your GitHub is proof of work. Anyone can say "I know how to code" on a resume. A GitHub full of real projects, with real commit history, shows that you actually do the work. That is worth more than any certification.

Claude Code: AI-Powered Development in Your Terminal

▶

Claude Code is not just another chatbot. It is an AI agent that lives in your terminal and can read your files, write code, run commands, and build entire features. It is what I used to build this entire portfolio site.

How it works: You open your terminal, type claude, and start talking to it like a collaborator. "Read the index.html and fix the mobile nav." It reads the file, understands the structure, writes the fix, and applies it. You review and approve.

What makes it different from the web chat:

It can see your entire project. It reads files, understands how they connect, and makes changes across multiple files at once. The web chat version of Claude has no idea what is in your codebase. Claude Code does.

It runs commands. It can install packages, run tests, check for errors, and deploy your site. It is not just suggesting code, it is actively building.

It remembers context. Within a session, Claude Code remembers everything you have discussed and every file it has read. It gets smarter about your specific project as the conversation continues.

This is the tool that makes vibe coding possible. You describe what you want in plain English. Claude Code builds it. You review, iterate, and ship. One person doing the work of a team.

Investing in the Right Model Tier

▶

Here is something I learned the hard way: the model tier you use directly determines the quality of what you can build.

Claude has different tiers. The free version (Haiku) is fine for quick questions and simple tasks. But if you are building a product, writing complex code, or doing anything that requires sustained reasoning over a large codebase, you need the Pro or Max plan with access to Opus or Sonnet.

Why the tier matters:

Cheaper models have weaker reasoning. They can write a function, but they struggle to understand how that function fits into the bigger system. They lose track of what they are doing on complex tasks.

Cheaper models have smaller context windows. They literally cannot hold your whole project in their head at once. For serious building, you need a model that can see the full picture.

Cheaper models produce more "slop." Generic code, generic copy, generic design. The kind of output that takes longer to fix than it would to write from scratch.

Think of it this way: you would not try to build a house with toy tools. Depending on your project, you may need to invest in a higher tier of Claude to get the reasoning power your work requires. A $20/month subscription that saves you 20 hours of work is the best deal in tech.

gstack: Garry Tan's 23-Skill Power Toolkit

▶

gstack is a framework built by Garry Tan (the CEO of Y Combinator) that turns Claude Code into a full engineering team. It adds 23 specialized "skills" that each act as a different role: CEO, designer, engineer, QA lead, security officer, and more.

Key skills:

/plan-ceo-review reviews your project plan from a founder perspective. It asks the hard questions about whether your idea solves a real problem.

/design-review audits your site with a designer's eye and actually fixes the issues it finds. Spacing, typography, color, responsiveness, the works.

/qa systematically tests your site, finds bugs, and fixes them with one commit per fix.

/ship handles the entire deployment process: tests, review, changelog, push, PR.

/investigate debugs errors systematically with root cause analysis instead of random guessing.

Why this matters: Solo builders used to have to wear every hat themselves, and most of those hats did not fit. gstack gives you expert-level review in every discipline. It is like having a startup team that works at 810x speed.

I used gstack's skills throughout building this portfolio and The Melody Method. /design-review caught visual issues I would have missed. /qa found bugs before users did. /ship made deployment painless. These are not toys. These are professional tools.

The Sprint Workflow: Think, Plan, Build, Review, Ship

▶

Professional software teams use sprints. Short, focused cycles where you plan what to build, build it, review it, and ship it. gstack brings this same discipline to solo building.

The workflow:

1. Think. What problem are you solving? Who is it for? What does success look like? Use /office-hours to brainstorm and validate your idea.

2. Plan. Break the work into small, shippable pieces. Use /plan-ceo-review to stress test your plan. Get the scope right before you write a line of code.

3. Build. Use Claude Code to write the code. Stay focused on one feature at a time. Commit often.

4. Review. Run /design-review for visual polish. Run /review for code quality. Run /qa to find bugs. Fix everything before moving on.

5. Ship. Use /ship to push it live. Write a changelog entry. Deploy. Then reflect on what went well and what to improve next time.

This is not about following a rigid process. It is about building the discipline to ship things that are actually finished, not half-done demos. The sprint workflow turns "I am working on it" into "it is live and people are using it."
